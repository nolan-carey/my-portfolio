www.simple-fin.com

My largest project to date simple-fin. Essentially simple-fin is my take on the perfect financial website. My end goal for this website is to host it publicly and to offer freemium access to the use of the website. The main architecture of the website it flask, with a mySQL database. It is distinguished between 5 sections (equity, derivatives, fixed income, news, retirement) with each heading having an intuitive subheading for navigation and user experience. Something in particular that I am proud of is the similarity algorithm for navigation in the main page search bar. The search bar takes the user input and using a Jaro-Winkler approach predicts what the user is wanting to look for.

For user experience I felt it was essential to create a database (mySql) that saves users stock picks and the date they added said stock, so for one the user can be notified if there are any major news articles, price changes etc, as well as so they can create a portfolio and do complex calculations such as portfolio correlation and etc…

Also, for user experience i added ways to download blance sheets, income statements and cash flows directly to Excel

I also wanted to make sure the website had modern approaches for calculating derivatives and retirement so below I have methods for derivative pricing such as Black Scholas, Binomial Modelling, Monte-Carlo Simulation

I am currently still working, adding, changing things on this website and the summary of simple-fin is not comphrensive so ask if you have any questions!

This was particularly challenging because it required integrating financial knowledge with practical coding skills. As I coded the application, I learned valuable lessons about the design of user interfaces, particularly in how to make complex information accessible. Ensuring that the stock price chart was clear and easy to read was a top priority. This meant not only fine-tuning the aesthetics, such as the color scheme and layout but also ensuring the responsiveness of the interface so that users could interact with the data effectively.



The experience of fetching and manipulating financial data, and then presenting it in a user-friendly format, was immensely rewarding. It honed my skills in Python and deepened my understanding of event-driven programming. More importantly, it taught me the importance of user-centered design in financial software development—a principle that I now appreciate is as crucial as any technical skill. The project was a significant first step in my journey to master GUI development with a focus on enhancing user experience.

Inverse Kinematics

This GUI for a 2 DOF (Degree of Freedom) robotic arm was an integral part of the design process for a cost-effective plasma cutting solution. The interface was crafted to simulate and visualize the arm's kinematics, specifically its range of motion and the positions it could achieve. The inputs for the X and Y positions, along with the lengths of the arms, allowed me to calculate the required angles (theta values) for the arm's joints to reach the desired location.



The development of this GUI was not just a programming exercise; it was a practical application of robotics principles. It enabled me to explore the physical constraints and capabilities of the robotic arm before the actual build, thus saving time, resources, and guiding the hardware design with insights gained from the software model.



By adjusting parameters such as arm lengths and target positions, I could immediately see the effects on the arm's configuration. The GUI provided a visual feedback loop that was essential for understanding the inverse kinematics involved – the complex calculations that determine joint angles based on the desired position of the arm's end effector.



This tool bridged the gap between theoretical robotics and real-world application. It allowed for rapid prototyping, ensuring that when I moved to the construction phase, I had a solid foundation of how the arm should perform. This pre-emptive approach was crucial, especially considering the precision required for plasma cutting tasks. The GUI was a testament to how software can inform and enhance the engineering design process, providing a clear path from concept to creation.

Robotic Cooking Pan

This embedded systems project showcases a creative and practical application in the form of a robotic cooking pan capable of flipping food automatically. It's a multifaceted endeavor that incorporates various aspects of mechanical and electrical engineering, along with computer-aided design (CAD), to bring to life a device that enhances the cooking experience.



The project included designing the physical structure in CAD software, which allowed for precise planning and visualization of the mechanical components such as the linear actuators and the framework that supports the flipping mechanism. The electrical schematics were integral in ensuring that the components like relays and actuators worked in tandem to control the movement of the pan efficiently.



In the prototype stage, the iterative process of testing and refining the design was crucial. It involved troubleshooting the hardware, refining the code for the microcontrollers, and ensuring that the actuators received the correct signals to perform the flipping action.



A key feature of this device is the embedded system control that coordinates the hardware components. The linear actuators, chosen for their precision and reliability, are the muscles of the device, while the relays act as the switches controlling the actuators’ power supply based on the signals from the embedded system.



Reflecting on the project, I see the potential for further enhancements. Integrating temperature sensors could provide a more responsive cooking experience by flipping the food when it reaches the optimal cooking temperature. Additionally, a timer setting could offer the user more control, allowing for precise cooking times for different types of food. These improvements would make the robotic cooking pan more autonomous and user-friendly, capable of accommodating a wider variety of cooking styles and preferences.



The experience gained from this project is invaluable, highlighting the intersection of mechanical design, electrical engineering, and embedded system programming. The skills learned here are not just applicable to the culinary world but can be transferred to other domains requiring automation and precision. If this project were to be revisited, the addition of sensors and enhanced user controls would represent the next step in its evolution, illustrating the iterative nature of engineering design. link: link to video

Computer Vision project

This image depicts a computer vision project that integrates a variety of technologies and skills to create a system capable of tracking faces and directing a servo-motor-controlled toy gun towards the detected position. It's a complex setup that serves as a practical example of applied robotics, combining elements of mechanical design, electronics, and software engineering.



The project involved designing parts in CAD software, which were then brought to life through 3D printing, showcasing the synergy between digital design and physical manufacturing. The mechanical housing and mounts for the servo motors were precisely calculated to ensure accurate movement and alignment with the vision system's output.



At the heart of the operation is an Arduino microcontroller, chosen for its accessibility and versatility. It interprets the data received from the computer vision algorithm—developed using the OpenCV library—and translates it into motion commands for the servo motors. This interplay between software and hardware is critical for real-time face tracking and the subsequent targeting mechanism.



The use of OpenCV for face detection highlights the power of computer vision techniques. OpenCV (Open Source Computer Vision Library) is a library of programming functions aimed at real-time computer vision, and in this project, it is used to process visual data and identify facial features.



Reflecting on the project, it is evident that a system like this could be enhanced with additional features such as motion prediction to improve tracking accuracy or safety mechanisms to ensure responsible usage. The integration of CAD design, 3D printing, microcontroller programming, and computer vision creates a robust framework for similar automated systems, potentially extending to applications beyond the scope of this prototype. This project not only sharpened technical skills across multiple domains but also provided a deep understanding of the interdisciplinary nature of robotics projects. link to video
